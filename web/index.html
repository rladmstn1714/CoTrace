Measuring, Inducing, and Exposing Goal-Level AI Contributions in Collaboration

CoTrace traces how humans and LLMs jointly shape goals, requirements, and decisions across multi-turn collaboration—not only who wrote the final artifact.

Eunsu Kim Jessica R. Mindel Kyungjin Kim Sherry Tongshuang Wu
KAIST · Carnegie Mellon University · Seoul National University
Paper Code

Why goal attribution matters

LLMs can propose structure, introduce constraints, ask questions, and make concrete decisions that shape a task before the final output exists. CoTrace makes those hidden contributions visible.

Beyond final artifacts

Instead of asking only who produced the final text, CoTrace follows how goals and requirements evolve through dialogue.

Direct + indirect influence

The framework captures explicit requirement creation as well as subtle influence, such as an AI question that prompts a later human decision.

Useful for reflection

Goal-level analysis can help users calibrate reliance, understand hidden AI work, and collaborate more intentionally.

The CoTrace pipeline

CoTrace decomposes collaboration into goals, requirements, actions, and influence links, then aggregates them into interpretable contribution scores.

Outcome & action extraction

Segment dialogue and identify outcomes, atomic actions, and whether each action shapes, executes, or plays another role.

Requirement extraction

Convert goals into independently checkable requirements and track create, revise, and delete operations over time.

Influence labeling

Link actions to requirements through direct or implicit influence, capturing both explicit decisions and subtle steering.

Contribution scoring

Aggregate influence across requirements and goal levels to summarize each participant's role in shaping the collaboration.

Key findings

The paper studies CoTrace as an evaluation suite, a design tool, and a reflection tool for human–AI collaboration.

Humans set direction; models fill in details.

Across real-world logs, humans dominated high-level goal shaping, while LLMs contributed more to specific child goals and concrete requirements—especially in technical tasks.

Interaction design changes AI initiative.

Simulations showed that prompting strategies and requiring messages before tool actions can increase assistant-originated requirements, making system design a lever for goal shaping.

More goal shaping is not automatically better.

Assistant-created requirements were often satisfied immediately, but increased model goal shaping did not clearly translate into higher overall output quality.

Users recalibrate after seeing CoTrace.

In a user study, participants revised their sense of who contributed what after viewing goal-level analyses of their own human–LLM conversations.

"I didn't necessarily make the micro decisions."

Participant reflection reported in the paper, motivating goal-level attribution for human–AI collaboration.
